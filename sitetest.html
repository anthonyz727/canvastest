Tech Support Best Practices

Welcome to the Team!

Welcome, intern and work-study students! You are now a vital part of the college's Technology Support team. This training hub is designed to introduce you to our best practices and prepare you for the common challenges you'll encounter.

Your Role is Critical

As a Technology Support Specialist, you are often the "face" of the IT department. Your interactions with students, faculty, and staff shape their perception of our entire team. Your goal isn't just to fix problems—it's to provide outstanding service, reduce frustration, and empower our users. This guide will help you do that.

How to Use This Hub

Use the navigation menu on the left to move through the modules. We recommend going in order, but feel free to jump to any section. Pay special attention to the "Scenarios" module to test your knowledge.

Remember: We don't expect you to know everything! The most important skill you can have is knowing when and how to ask for help.

Core Principles

These are the non-negotiable foundations of our support philosophy.

1. Empathy & Patience

Users are almost always frustrated by the time they contact us. They aren't angry at *you*, they're angry at the situation.

  • Listen without interrupting.
  • Use phrases like, "I understand that must be frustrating."
  • Stay calm, even if the user is upset.

2. Clear Communication

Avoid technical jargon. Our users are professors, artists, and students, not IT experts.

  • Say "restart the computer" not "power-cycle the unit."
  • Explain *what* you're doing in simple terms.
  • Confirm they understand the solution before you end the interaction.

3. Privacy & FERPA

This is a critical legal and ethical responsibility. You will have access to sensitive information.

  • NEVER look at files, emails, grades, or personal info unrelated to the problem.
  • NEVER share student or staff information with anyone.
  • Always log out of user accounts when you are finished.
  • If you see something sensitive, say nothing and forget it.

4. Ownership & Follow-Up

When you take a ticket, you "own" it until it's resolved or escalated.

  • Don't let a ticket sit without updates.
  • If you're waiting on a user, make a note and set a reminder.
  • If you escalate a ticket, provide clear, detailed notes.

The Support Workflow

Follow these steps for every single ticket, every single time. This ensures consistency and quality.

1

Triage: Listen & Gather Info

Before you try to fix *anything*, you must understand the problem. Ask the "WH" questions.

  • Who: Who is affected? (Just them, or a whole office?)
  • What: What is the *exact* problem? What is the error message?
  • Where: Where are they? (Office, classroom, home?) What is the device?
  • When: When did it start? Is it consistent?
  • Tried: What have you already tried? (Saves you from repeating steps).
2

Troubleshoot: Analyze & Fix

Start with the simplest solution first. Use your tools.

  • Check the Obvious: Is it plugged in? Is it turned on? Is Wi-Fi on?
  • "Have you tried...": ...restarting it? This fixes 50% of problems.
  • Consult the Knowledge Base (KB): Search our KB *first*. Someone has likely seen this before.
  • Isolate the Problem: Can they log in on another machine? Can *you* log in on theirs?
3

Document: Record Everything

If it's not in the ticket, it didn't happen. Good notes are essential for your teammates and for tracking trends.

  • Write clear, concise notes in the ticketing system.
  • Include the problem, the steps you took, and the final resolution.
  • If you couldn't fix it, document what you tried before escalating.
4

Escalate: Ask for Help

This is not failure. It is efficiency. Do not spend more than 15-20 minutes on a problem you're not making progress on.

  • Politely tell the user, "I'm going to bring in a senior specialist to look at this."
  • Escalate the ticket in the system with your detailed notes.
  • Ask the senior tech to explain the solution to you so you learn for next time!

Common Issues (The "Big 5")

You will see these problems every single day. Here's your quick-start guide.

1. "I can't connect to the Wi-Fi."
  • Ask: Are you connecting to `College-Secure` or `College-Guest`? (Faculty/staff/students should *always* use `College-Secure`).
  • Action: Have them "Forget" the network on their device.
  • Action: Re-connect to `College-Secure`. Ensure they use their main college username and password (not email address).
  • Escalate if: The device still won't connect (may need driver or hardware check) or their password isn't working.
2. "I need my password reset."
  • Policy: We *must* verify their identity. Ask for their College ID card (in person) or follow phone verification protocol (ask your supervisor!).
  • Tool: Use the "Active Directory" (or other password) tool.
  • Action: Set a temporary password (e.g., `Welcome2025!`).
  • Action: Instruct them to log in *immediately* and change it. Stay with them until it's confirmed working.
  • Action: Direct them to the self-service password reset portal to set up recovery questions for next time.
3. "I can't print."
  • Triage: Is it just them or the whole office? (If multiple, check the printer itself for errors/paper jams).
  • Triage: Are they on the correct network? (`College-Secure` is required for network printers).
  • Action: Check if the printer is installed. (Control Panel > Devices and Printers).
  • Action: Is the correct printer set as the default?
  • Action: If it's a "Find-Me" or "PaperCut" queue, do they have print funds?
  • Action: Try removing and re-adding the printer from the print server.
4. "The projector in my classroom won't turn on."

This is an URGENT issue. A class is being disrupted.

  • Action: Get the Building and Room Number.
  • Action: Ask them to press "On" or "Display On" on the wall/podium control panel. (It sounds simple, but it's the most common fix).
  • Action: Ask them to check if the correct input is selected (e.g., "Podium PC," "Laptop/HDMI").
  • Escalate: If that doesn't work, escalate *immediately* to the Classroom AV team or go there yourself if it's your job. Do not try complex troubleshooting over the phone.
5. "I can't log in to [Canvas/Blackboard/LMS]."
  • Triage: Is their password working for *other* college systems (like email)?
  • If Yes (Password is OK): The problem is with their LMS account. This is usually an enrollment issue. Escalate this ticket to the `LMS-Admin` or `Registrar` queue. We (Tech Support) can't fix this.
  • If No (Password Fails Everywhere): This is a password reset issue. Go back to Common Issue #2.

Scenario 1: The Panicked Professor

A professor calls, sounding very stressed. "My class starts in 2 minutes, I have 100 students here, and the classroom computer won't turn on! You need to fix this NOW!"

Best Practice:

  • Stay Calm & Reassure: "I understand. Let's get this fixed for you. I will escalate this immediately."
  • Triage (Fast!): Get the Building and Room Number.
  • Action: This is a "Classroom Down" emergency. Escalate it *immediately* to the AV/Classroom team. Create an URGENT ticket.
  • What Not To Do: Don't try to walk them through checking power cables or rebooting for 10 minutes. An in-person tech needs to go *now*.

Scenario 2: The Personal Laptop

A student comes to the help desk with their personal laptop. "I think I have a virus, and my midterm paper is on here! Can you just run your tools and get rid of it for me?"

Best Practice:

  • Empathize & State Policy: "I know how scary that is. Unfortunately, our college policy prevents us from working on personally-owned devices due to liability."
  • Help How You Can: "What I *can* do is help you get connected to the `College-Secure` Wi-Fi, and I can point you to free antivirus tools like Malwarebytes or Avast that you can download and run yourself."
  • Offer Alternatives: "I can also show you how to log into our campus computer lab machines, so you can work on your paper there while your laptop is being repaired."

Scenario 3: The "I Don't Know" User

You get a ticket that just says "computer broken." You call the user, and they say, "I don't know, it's just not working right. It's slow. Can you just fix it?"

Best Practice:

  • Be Patient & Triage: This is where your triage questions are key. Don't give up.
  • Ask Specifics: "I can definitely help. When you say it's slow, what are you doing? Is it slow when you open a web browser? Or when you open a Word document?"
  • Get Context: "When did this start?" "Have you restarted the computer recently?"
  • Action: If they are on campus, schedule a time to visit or remote in. A vague "slow" complaint is often too many browser tabs, a pending update, or a failing hard drive. You'll need to investigate.

Our Tools

This is your primary toolkit. Get familiar with where to find them.

1. Ticketing System (e.g., Zendesk, ServiceNow)

This is your brain and your to-do list. Every request for help *must* become a ticket. You will log in here first thing every day. This is how we track our work, see trends, and manage escalations.

2. Knowledge Base (KB) (e.g., Confluence, SharePoint)

Your new best friend. Before you ask a teammate, search the KB. It contains step-by-step guides for 90% of our common issues, like "How to Map a Network Printer" or "New Faculty Setup Checklist."

3. Remote Desktop Tool (e.g., TeamViewer, RemotePC)

This tool lets you see and control a user's computer (with their permission!) to troubleshoot problems without leaving your desk. Always ask permission before you click or change anything.

4. Active Directory (AD) Users & Computers

This is the directory of all users and computers on campus. You will primarily use this to reset passwords and unlock accounts. This is a powerful tool—treat it with care.
